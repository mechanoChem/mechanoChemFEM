2  * @page diffusion_reaction Example 1 : Turing patterns: diffusion-reaction systems
3  * \section Introduction
4  * We solve two diffusion reaction equations:
5  * \f[
6  \frac{\partial C_\text{1}}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{j}_1=r_1
7 \f]
8 *\f[
9 \frac{\partial C_\text{2}}{\partial t}+\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{j}_2=r_2
10 \f]
11 * where \f$\boldsymbol{j}_1 \f$ and \f$\boldsymbol{j}_2 \f$ are flux terms:
12  * \f[
13  \boldsymbol{j}_1=-M_1\nabla C_\text{1}; \quad \boldsymbol{j}_2=-M_2\nabla C_\text{2}\\
14 \f]
15 *\f$r_1\f$ and \f$r_2\f$ are reaction terms:
16  * \f[
17  r_1= R_{10}+R_{11}C_1+R_{13}C_1^2C_2; \quad r_1= R_{20}+R_{21}C_1^2C_2
18 \f]
19 The boundary condiiton is
20 * \f[
21 \boldsymbol{j}_1\cdot\boldsymbol{n}=j_n \text{ on }\Gamma_2;\quad \quad
22 \boldsymbol{j}_1\cdot\boldsymbol{n}=0 \text{ on }\Gamma \backslash \Gamma_2; \quad \quad
23  \boldsymbol{j}_2\cdot\boldsymbol{n}=0 \text{ on }\Gamma
24 \f]
25 The coupled diffusion-reaction equations for two species follow Schnakenberg kinetics.
26 For an activator-inhibitor species pair, these equations use auto-inhibition with cross-activation of a short range species, and auto-activation with cross-inhibition of a long range species to form so-called Turing patterns.
